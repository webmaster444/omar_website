Specifying and measuring application behaviour is our thing, but the behaviour of the humans on a project is worth considering too..

Where one stands depends on where they sit. Customers, business analysts, developers and testers traditionally ask different questions of the software we're building. Conventional industry thinking sees these as gaps.

The most infamous might be the gap between business-heads and tech-heads:

Any one of these smart people could have a relevant insight at any time. Modern experience finds the task of devising specifications works best when all interested parties are involved from the outset.

We also find that a more nimble and thoughtful approach to specification levers huge benefits to software projects

Specification by example helps close communication gaps between various project participants, forming a tendency to speak the same language and build a consistent understanding of the domain

We deliberately promote conversations about functional behaviours and about their desired business impacts

All considerations are potentially valid. We think it shouldn’t only be the job of one 'stakeholder’ to devise requirements. This task can be split out between all parties who have a stake/interest.

So we deliberately promote conversations about application behaviours and desired business impacts.

The process of specifying behaviours via examples:

- Focuses attention - no more vague requirement lists

- Begets more examples and sparks ideas

- Gets team members speaking the same language - bridging communication gaps

A simple process which leads to higher performing, happier teams
